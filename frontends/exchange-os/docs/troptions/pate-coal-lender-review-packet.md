## Recommended Financing Routes

### Routes currently CONDITIONAL (can proceed if documents are provided)

#### 1. Diligence Bridge
- **Status:** CONDITIONAL
- **What it is:** Short-term capital (typically 6–18 months) to fund the diligence process — title search, attorney review, environmental assessment, and updated QP report — so the asset can reach a full financing round.
- **Typical amount:** $250,000–$1,500,000 depending on diligence scope
- **Requirements to activate:** Deed + mineral rights deed + basic title review + borrower entity documentation
- **Why it fits now:** The technical evidence is strong. A diligence bridge requires less paperwork than a full mineral lender loan.
- **Recommended path:** Submit deed and mineral rights deed → engage mining attorney for title opinion → fund diligence costs via bridge → pursue full financing.

#### 2. Operator JV
- **Status:** CONDITIONAL
- **What it is:** A mining operator provides capital, equipment, and permitting expertise in exchange for a revenue share, working interest, or equity stake in the coal operation.
- **Requirements:** Deed, mineral rights deed, permit status, legal opinion, and an operator letter of intent.
- **Advantage:** Operators can sometimes fund their own diligence rather than requiring the owner to fund it upfront.

#### 3. Royalty / Streaming Finance
- **Status:** CONDITIONAL
- **What it is:** A royalty financier pays a lump sum today in exchange for a percentage of future coal revenue (royalty) or the right to purchase coal at a fixed price (streaming).
- **Requirements:** Deed, mineral rights deed, title opinion, updated QP report, and sometimes offtake or operator LOI.
- **Typical structure:** 2–5% gross revenue royalty in exchange for $1M–$10M upfront.

### Routes currently BLOCKED

#### 4. Private Mineral Lender
- **Status:** BLOCKED
- **Reason:** No title documents, no legal opinion, no updated QP report. Private mineral lenders require clean title before advancing secured debt.
- **Path to unblock:** Submit deed → chain of title → title opinion → updated QP report → legal opinion → lender term sheet.

#### 5. Aave / DeFi Collateral
- **Status:** BLOCKED (permanent hard block)
- **Reason:** Raw, unencumbered coal and mineral-rights interests are not accepted as collateral on Aave or any current DeFi lending protocol. Aave only accepts on-chain liquid tokens that it has approved as collateral. A raw mineral-rights deed is not tokenized, not liquid, and not on Aave's approved collateral list.
- **Note:** This hard block survives even if all 22 documents are submitted. There is no path through this route for PATE-COAL-001 under current DeFi protocol rules.

#### 6. XRPL Permissioned Receipt
- **Status:** BLOCKED (Phase 3 — deferred)
- **Reason:** XRPL IOU issuance requires a complete legal wrapper, authorized XRPL trustline, issuer policy, lender terms, and PATE-COAL-001 specific authorization.
- **When this could unlock:** After Phase 3 — title clear, legal opinion complete, lender terms agreed, XRPL issuer policy filed, authorized trustlines established.
- **What it is not:** A XRPL receipt is a permissioned record of a lender transaction. It is not a public token sale, public stablecoin, or liquid DeFi asset.

**Why reported in-place value ($8.37B) does not equal lender advance value:**

| Factor | Effect on lender value |
|---|---|
| In-place vs. recoverable | Only a fraction of coal in-place is economically recoverable |
| Extraction cost | Mining cost, royalties, reclamation bonds reduce net value |
| Market price volatility | Thermal coal prices have declined; metallurgical coal more stable |
| Permitting risk | Tennessee permitting timeline and SMCRA bond requirements |
| Title risk | Without clean title, lenders cannot foreclose |
| Liquidity discount | Mineral rights are not liquid assets |
| Typical haircut | Lenders commonly advance 10–30% of engineer-assessed recoverable value on mineral-rights assets |
